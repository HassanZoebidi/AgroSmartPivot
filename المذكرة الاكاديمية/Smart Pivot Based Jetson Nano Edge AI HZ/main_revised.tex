\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This dissertation presents the design, implementation, and field evaluation of a smart centre-pivot irrigation system that exploits edge artificial intelligence (AI) to enable real-time, spatially resolved, and connectivity-independent variable-rate irrigation under semi-arid conditions. The system, hereafter referred to as the \emph{Smart Pivot}, integrates an NVIDIA Jetson Nano embedded computing module, a Sony IMX219 RGB camera, a heterogeneous sensor suite, and an optimised YOLOv8 neural-network inference pipeline to perform continuous in-field detection of crop canopy, weed pressure, bare soil, and plant water-stress indicators as the pivot lateral traverses the irrigated circle.

The principal motivation for this work is the chronic spatial mismatch between the water demand of different sub-regions of an irrigated field and the uniform delivery imposed by conventional centre-pivot systems. By deploying deep-learning inference directly at the edge---on the moving pivot itself---the Smart Pivot replaces schedule-driven, fixed-rate irrigation with a demand-driven, spatially resolved prescription computed continuously and without dependence on cloud connectivity. A complementary Android application, based on TensorFlow Lite, provides farm operators with a portable on-site diagnostic tool.

The principal technical contributions of this work are fourfold: (i)~a fully documented five-stage quantisation pipeline converting a trained PyTorch YOLOv8n checkpoint into a TensorRT FP16 engine that achieves 31~fps on the Jetson Nano Maxwell GPU with less than $0.5$~mAP@0.5 degradation relative to the full-precision baseline; (ii)~a zone-aggregation and fuzzy rule-based decision logic that fuses per-frame detections with soil-moisture and evapotranspiration data to generate variable-rate sprinkler commands; (iii)~a solar-powered, IP65-protected hardware integration providing full energy autonomy under the climatic conditions of north-eastern Algeria; and (iv)~a cloud monitoring dashboard based on MQTT, InfluxDB, and Grafana that decouples farm observability from real-time control.

Field evaluation over a single growing season yielded water savings of 18--23\% relative to a conventional fixed-rate baseline, with an end-to-end perception-to-actuation latency of 38~ms. These results demonstrate the practical feasibility of edge-AI precision irrigation for water-constrained semi-arid agriculture and provide a reference architecture for future research in AIoT-driven smart farming.

\section{Background and Motivation}

Global agriculture faces compounding pressures that call for a fundamental rethinking of how land and water resources are managed. The United Nations projects that the world population will reach approximately 9.7~billion by 2050~\cite{FAO2020}, requiring an estimated 70~percent increase in food production relative to current levels. This challenge must be met against a backdrop of accelerating freshwater scarcity: agriculture accounts for roughly 70~percent of global freshwater withdrawals~\cite{FAO2020}, yet irrigated land, which produces approximately 40~percent of the world's food on only 20~percent of the cultivated area, is highly sensitive to supply variability.

The Mediterranean basin and the semi-arid regions of North Africa, including Algeria, are among the areas most severely affected by these trends. Recurring multi-year droughts, declining aquifer levels, and high evapotranspiration rates combine to make each unit of irrigation water strategically important. In the semi-arid plateaux surrounding El Oued and Constantine, supplemental irrigation is a structural requirement for cereal, vegetable, and fodder production. Improving the efficiency of that irrigation is therefore directly linked to regional food security, rural livelihoods, and the long-term sustainability of water resources.

Among irrigation technologies, the \abbr{centre-pivot}{CP} system has emerged as the dominant method for large open-field agriculture worldwide owing to its relatively high application efficiency, low labour requirements, and compatibility with mechanisation compared with surface methods~\cite{Evans2012}. A centre-pivot consists of a long lateral pipe fitted with sprinkler heads that rotates about a fixed central tower to irrigate a circular field. However, in their conventional configuration, centre-pivots apply water uniformly along the lateral regardless of within-field spatial variability in soil texture, crop vigour, weed pressure, or micro-topography. This uniform application creates a persistent mismatch between the spatially heterogeneous demand of the crop and the spatially uniform supply of the system, simultaneously wasting water and limiting yield potential~\cite{Pierce1999}.

\abbr{Variable-Rate Irrigation}{VRI} was developed specifically to address this limitation by enabling independent control of individual sprinkler banks as the lateral rotates. However, VRI requires spatially resolved, continuously updated information about the field state to be effective---a requirement that conventional soil-moisture probe networks, sparsely deployed by necessity, are structurally unable to fulfil. This information gap has positioned VRI as a natural target for the broader paradigm of \abbr{Agriculture 4.0}{Ag4.0}, in which the \abbr{Internet of Things}{IoT} and \abbr{Artificial Intelligence}{AI} jointly transform conventional fields into data-rich, decision-aware production systems~\cite{Liu2020}.

Recent advances in three converging areas have made a step-change in on-pivot intelligence possible. First, \abbr{Edge AI}{EAI} platforms such as the NVIDIA Jetson Nano deliver hundreds of GFLOPS of GPU-accelerated inference within a 5--10~W power envelope and at a unit cost accessible to smallholders~\cite{Shi2016}. Second, the \abbr{You Only Look Once}{YOLO} family of single-stage object detectors---and specifically YOLOv8 (Ultralytics, 2023) and its successor YOLOv11 (Ultralytics, 2024)~\cite{Jocher2023,Ultralytics2024}---achieve real-time agricultural object detection with state-of-the-art accuracy. Third, optimisation toolchains such as NVIDIA TensorRT enable the deployment of FP16 and INT8 quantised models on constrained embedded GPUs with negligible accuracy degradation~\cite{Jacob2018}. The convergence of these three elements opens a new design space: a pivot-mounted edge-AI system capable of continuous visual perception, autonomous irrigation decision-making, and full operation without reliance on cloud connectivity.

\section{Problem Statement}

Despite considerable progress in precision agriculture, the irrigation decisions taken on most farms remain largely uniform and only weakly informed by real-time spatial data. Existing smart-irrigation solutions face a structural spatial-resolution gap: the handful of soil-moisture probes typically deployed capture temporal dynamics at their measurement locations but fail to represent the within-field heterogeneity that VRI is designed to exploit. Meanwhile, cloud-based computer-vision approaches, although technically capable of high spatial resolution, are poorly suited to the operational realities of rural Algeria and similar semi-arid regions, where Internet connectivity is unreliable, latency is incompatible with closed-loop pivot control, and continuous video upload is financially prohibitive for smallholders.

More specifically, the following gaps motivate this research:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{G\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2em]
    \item Conventional centre-pivot systems apply water uniformly and therefore cannot exploit within-field spatial variability in soil moisture, crop vigour, or weed pressure.
    \item Existing smart-irrigation solutions rely predominantly on sparse point sensors, capturing insufficient spatial information for fine-grained variable-rate decision-making.
    \item Cloud-based AI architectures for irrigation are ill-suited to arid and semi-arid regions where rural Internet connectivity is unreliable and costly.
    \item State-of-the-art object detectors such as YOLOv8 and YOLOv11 have not been systematically deployed on low-cost edge platforms for closed-loop centre-pivot control.
    \item There is a paucity of independent evidence on the detection performance, energy footprint, and water-saving potential of edge-AI smart pivots under the climatic conditions of Algeria.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Questions}

This dissertation addresses the following specific research questions:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*.}, leftmargin=2em]
    \item How can YOLOv8 be adapted and optimised to achieve real-time inference at agronomically acceptable accuracy on a Jetson Nano mounted on a centre-pivot irrigation system?
    \item Which agronomically meaningful field-state indicators---weed density, canopy cover, growth stage, and visible water stress---can be reliably detected from pivot-mounted imagery under realistic semi-arid field conditions?
    \item How can the output of an on-edge inference pipeline be translated into variable-rate irrigation commands and communicated to the pivot's actuation system within a latency budget compatible with real-time control?
    \item What quantitative advantages does an edge-AI smart-pivot architecture provide over conventional fixed-rate pivots and cloud-based alternatives with respect to latency, bandwidth, energy consumption, and operational robustness?
    \item To what extent can the proposed solution be considered technically feasible and economically accessible for smallholders and research institutions in regions such as El Oued, Algeria?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Aims and Objectives}

The overarching aim of this research is to design, implement, and evaluate a smart centre-pivot irrigation system that deploys an optimised YOLOv8 computer-vision pipeline on a Jetson Nano embedded board to enable real-time, spatially resolved, and connectivity-independent variable-rate irrigation under semi-arid conditions. The following specific objectives operationalise this aim:

\begin{itemize}
    \item[\textbf{O1.}] Critically survey and synthesise the literature on precision agriculture, smart irrigation, AIoT architectures, edge versus cloud AI deployment, and relevant sensing and detection technologies.
    \item[\textbf{O2.}] Define the agronomic and engineering requirements for a smart centre-pivot deployable in semi-arid Algerian conditions, and develop a hardware and software architecture that satisfies those requirements.
    \item[\textbf{O3.}] Collect, curate, and annotate a dataset of pivot-relevant agricultural imagery suitable for training a four-class YOLOv8 object-detection model.
    \item[\textbf{O4.}] Train, validate, and optimise YOLOv8n and YOLOv11n models on this dataset, and develop a five-stage TensorRT quantisation pipeline achieving real-time inference on the Jetson Nano.
    \item[\textbf{O5.}] Implement a complete closed-loop prototype integrating the inference pipeline, sensor-fusion decision logic, GPIO-based actuation, and cloud telemetry.
    \item[\textbf{O6.}] Evaluate the prototype through field experimentation with respect to detection accuracy, inference latency, energy efficiency, water savings, communication bandwidth, and operational robustness.
\end{itemize}

\section{Scope and Limitations}

The scope of this work is confined to centre-pivot irrigation of open-field arable crops under arid or semi-arid conditions. The primary reference context is Algeria, with the semi-arid regions of north-eastern Algeria and the broader El Oued governorate informing the design constraints. Drip irrigation, greenhouse cultivation, and sub-surface systems are outside the scope of the study, although several methodological contributions are transferable.

The edge-compute platform evaluated is exclusively the NVIDIA Jetson Nano (B01, 4~GB). The detection models evaluated are YOLOv8n (Ultralytics, 2023) and YOLOv11n (Ultralytics, 2024) in their nano configurations. The following pre-defined limitations are acknowledged: (i)~the field-collected dataset covers a single crop species and a limited range of weed species; (ii)~the field evaluation spans a single growing season at a single study site; (iii)~detection performance degrades under extreme illumination conditions (direct noon sunlight, dust storms, and nocturnal operation); and (iv)~the water-saving quantification relies on a counterfactual comparison rather than a parallel controlled experiment.

\section{Significance of the Research}

The significance of this work spans four complementary dimensions. \emph{Scientifically}, it contributes a documented, end-to-end reference architecture for pivot-mounted edge-AI irrigation control---a design space that remains largely uncharted in the published literature. \emph{Practically}, it demonstrates an affordable, deployable system that removes dependence on cloud infrastructure, making precision irrigation accessible at the smallholder scale in regions poorly served by Internet connectivity. \emph{Environmentally}, spatially resolved just-in-time water application reduces groundwater overdraft, pump-energy consumption, and water-quality degradation caused by runoff and deep percolation. \emph{Regionally}, the project contributes to the national agenda for agricultural modernisation in Algeria, where water security is a matter of strategic importance.

\section{Precision Agriculture}

\abbr{Precision Agriculture}{PA}, often used interchangeably with precision farming and site-specific crop management, is defined as a management strategy that employs spatial and temporal data to optimise agricultural inputs---water, fertilisers, pesticides, and energy---in accordance with the actual and localised needs of the crop, rather than applying them uniformly across an entire field~\cite{Pierce1999}. The theoretical underpinning of PA is that within-field variability in soil properties, crop development, and pest pressure is agronomically significant and that, by managing this variability explicitly, both input efficiency and yield can be improved simultaneously.

The history of agriculture is commonly framed in four developmental eras. Agriculture 1.0 refers to pre-industrial, labour-intensive subsistence practices. Agriculture 2.0 encompasses the mechanisation and agrochemical revolution of the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries. Agriculture 3.0 emerged from the 1980s onwards with the adoption of GPS, GIS, yield monitors, and early variable-rate technologies. Agriculture 4.0, the current frontier, is characterised by the convergence of IoT, big data analytics, cloud computing, robotics, and AI into integrated, cyber-physical agricultural systems~\cite{Liu2020}.

Despite four decades of research and development, PA adoption remains geographically uneven. Variable-rate technology, auto-steer machinery, and remote-sensing-based canopy analysis are routine on large farms in North America, Australia, and northern Europe, yet remain exceptional in most of Africa, the Middle East, and large parts of Asia. The barriers are well documented~\cite{Kamilaris2018}: high capital cost, lack of technical support infrastructure, the absence of locally calibrated models, and the implicit assumption in much commercial PA software that reliable broadband connectivity is available. Addressing these barriers by designing low-cost, connectivity-independent, and locally replicable systems is among the principal motivations of the present work.

% ─── 2.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Smart Irrigation Systems}

Irrigation systems are broadly classified into surface (flood and furrow), overhead sprinkler, micro-irrigation (drip and micro-sprinkler), and sub-surface drip configurations. Among overhead systems, the centre-pivot has become the global standard for irrigating large open-field crops owing to its high application uniformity, low operational labour, and compatibility with agrochemical and fertigation injection~\cite{Evans2012}.

A smart irrigation system is one in which the timing and quantity of water applied are determined automatically from sensor data and computational models rather than from a fixed operator schedule. The irrigation scheduling literature describes several methodological approaches~\cite{Tzounis2017}: (i)~\emph{soil-moisture-based scheduling}, where irrigation is triggered when a soil-moisture threshold is crossed; (ii)~\emph{evapotranspiration-based scheduling}, where crop water demand is estimated from meteorological data using reference ET models such as FAO-56 Penman-Monteith; (iii)~\emph{plant-based scheduling}, which uses direct physiological indicators such as stem-water potential or canopy temperature; (iv)~\emph{model-based scheduling}, incorporating crop simulation models; and (v)~\emph{data-driven hybrid scheduling}, which increasingly applies machine learning to fuse heterogeneous data streams into irrigation decisions.

Variable-Rate Irrigation constitutes a spatial extension of smart scheduling. Rather than computing a single irrigation depth for the whole field, VRI produces a spatially varying prescription map. This prescription is most naturally enacted on centre-pivot and linear-move systems, where individual sprinkler banks can be independently duty-cycled as the lateral traverses. Commercial VRI systems are available from major pivot manufacturers, but their uptake in arid and semi-arid regions of Africa and the southern Mediterranean remains limited~\cite{Evans2012}. In most installed systems, the VRI prescription is derived from pre-season soil-survey management zones rather than from real-time, per-revolution sensing---a limitation that the present work directly addresses.

% ─── 2.3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Internet of Things in Agriculture}

The IoT, in the agricultural context, denotes the network of physical sensing, actuation, and computing devices that collect, communicate, and act upon data from the agricultural environment~\cite{Atzori2010,Tzounis2017}. Agricultural IoT deployments are typically organised into three or four functional layers: (i)~the \emph{device layer}, hosting sensors and actuators; (ii)~the \emph{connectivity layer}, transporting data using protocols matched to the range and energy constraints of the application; (iii)~the optional \emph{edge or fog layer}, providing local aggregation and inference; and (iv)~the \emph{cloud layer}, providing large-scale storage, analytics, and integration with farm-management platforms.

In terms of connectivity, agricultural IoT most commonly exploits \abbr{Low-Power Wide-Area Network}{LPWAN} protocols---particularly LoRaWAN, NB-IoT, and Sigfox---because of their ability to cover large field areas with battery-powered nodes. Wi-Fi and 4G/5G provide complementary capabilities for higher-bandwidth applications such as video streaming~\cite{Muhammed2024}. A persistent challenge in deploying IoT for irrigation in developing regions is the absence of reliable Internet infrastructure, which limits the utility of architectures that depend on cloud connectivity for real-time decision-making. The edge-centric architecture proposed in this dissertation directly addresses this constraint.

% ─── 2.5 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Machine Learning for Irrigation Decision-Making}

\subsection{Regression and Classical Machine Learning}

Statistical and classical ML methods---linear regression, support vector regression, random forests, and gradient boosting---have been widely applied to predict reference evapotranspiration (ET$_0$), crop coefficients ($K_c$), and soil-moisture dynamics from weather and soil data~\cite{Kamilaris2018}. These methods are computationally inexpensive, interpretable, and often sufficient for ET-driven irrigation scheduling at field scale. Their principal limitation for the present application is an inability to ingest unstructured visual data and a consequent dependence on point-sensor networks for spatial awareness.

\subsection{Deep Learning and Temporal Modelling}

Recurrent neural architectures---in particular \abbr{Long Short-Term Memory}{LSTM} and \abbr{Gated Recurrent Unit}{GRU} networks---have demonstrated strong performance in modelling the temporal dynamics of soil moisture, crop water demand, and meteorological variables~\cite{Kamilaris2018}. Hybrid CNN-LSTM architectures, which combine spatial feature extraction with temporal sequence modelling, have further improved prediction accuracy when sufficient labelled time-series data are available. In the context of this project, temporal deep learning contributes the simplified Penman-Monteith ET estimation embedded in the decision logic, while the primary perception modality is computer vision.

\subsection{Computer Vision and YOLO-Family Detectors}
\label{subsec:yolo_background}

Computer vision---specifically deep \abbr{Convolutional Neural Network}{CNN}-based approaches---has become the dominant methodology for agronomic image analysis, including weed detection, disease identification, growth-stage classification, and yield estimation~\cite{Kamilaris2018}. For real-time object detection in the field, the YOLO family of single-stage detectors has emerged as the de facto standard owing to its favourable trade-off between inference speed and detection accuracy. The original YOLO architecture was proposed by Redmon et al.~\cite{Redmon2016} in 2016 and has been continuously refined through YOLOv2--v5, YOLOv7, YOLOv8 (Ultralytics, 2023), and YOLOv11 (Ultralytics, 2024). For agricultural applications specifically, YOLOv8 has become a widely validated reference architecture, with documented deployments in weed detection, fruit counting, and plant phenotyping.

\subsection{Federated Learning}

Federated learning~\cite{McMahan2017} is a distributed ML paradigm in which a global model is trained collaboratively across multiple decentralised clients without requiring the clients to share raw data. In the agricultural context, federated learning is particularly attractive for multi-farm deployments where data-sovereignty constraints preclude centralised data aggregation. Although federated learning is not implemented in the present prototype, it is identified as the natural mechanism for cross-farm model improvement once a fleet of Smart Pivot units is operational, and is discussed further in Section~\ref{sec:future}.

% ─── 2.7 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Identified Research Gaps}
\label{sec:gap}

The preceding review demonstrates that the constituent technologies of a smart centre-pivot driven by edge AI are individually mature, yet their systematic integration remains largely unaddressed in the published literature. Specifically:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{Gap \arabic*.}]
    \item Most published smart-irrigation systems rely on a small number of point sensors, capturing temporal dynamics well but providing inadequate spatial coverage for high-resolution VRI prescriptions.
    \item Where computer vision has been applied to agriculture, it has predominantly been deployed in offline UAV-based pipelines or cloud-centric architectures, not within closed-loop, on-pivot control loops.
    \item Prior work on YOLO-family detectors in agriculture has largely used earlier architectures (YOLOv2--v5) in isolation, without integration into irrigation control systems or field hardware.
    \item Comprehensive end-to-end evaluations combining detection accuracy, real-time inference latency on constrained edge hardware, energy consumption, and agronomic water-saving quantification in a single study remain rare.
    \item No published study---to the authors' knowledge---reports such an integrated evaluation under the agroclimatic conditions of semi-arid Algeria or comparable southern Mediterranean environments.
\end{enumerate}

This dissertation directly addresses Gaps 1--5 and makes the first documented contribution in Gap 5.

\section{Overall System Architecture}

The Smart Pivot follows the four-layer AIoT reference model~\cite{Muhammed2024}: (i)~the \emph{Device Layer}, hosting sensors and actuators; (ii)~the \emph{Edge Layer}, centred on the Jetson Nano and responsible for all real-time control; (iii)~the \emph{Connectivity Layer}, managing both intra-pivot communication and external telemetry; and (iv)~the \emph{Cloud Layer}, providing supervisory observability without constituting a bottleneck for control.

The decisive architectural principle is \emph{edge-first intelligence}: the Jetson Nano mounted on the pivot lateral executes every function required for safe and effective irrigation---vision inference, sensor fusion, decision logic, and actuation---entirely locally, without any dependency on an external network. Cloud connectivity, when available, provides non-critical farm-level observability. Four principles guided the architecture:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Edge-first intelligence:} all safety- and yield-critical computations execute on the Jetson Nano.
    \item \textbf{Energy autonomy:} the complete edge subsystem must operate from a field-deployable photovoltaic installation.
    \item \textbf{Graceful degradation:} failure or unavailability of any layer above the edge layer must not impair real-time irrigation control.
    \item \textbf{Openness and reproducibility:} all hardware is commercially available and all software is open-source, facilitating replication by other research groups and local farmers.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Sensor Subsystem}

The sensor subsystem combines a high-bandwidth visual channel with low-bandwidth point sensors to enable multi-modal data fusion. The Sony IMX219 camera interfaces through the MIPI CSI-2 port of the Jetson Nano, delivering hardware-accelerated colour space conversion and providing frames at 1080p/30~fps with sub-frame latency. The downward tilt of 30° from horizontal and the mounting height of 2.5~m above the crop canopy produce a ground-level footprint of approximately 1.5--2.0~m width, which is well matched to the inter-sprinkler spacing of the actuation system.

Capacitive soil-moisture probes are used in preference to resistive types because of their superior immunity to electrode corrosion and salinity---both persistent concerns in the saline-calcareous soils of the Algerian semi-arid zone~\cite{Tzounis2017}. Two probes are deployed at user-defined reference zones; their readings provide the soil-moisture component of the per-zone state vector and serve as a calibration anchor for the visual water-stress detection.

\subsection{Actuation Subsystem}

Eight independently controlled sprinkler banks are switched via 12~V latching solenoid valves driven from Jetson Nano GPIO pins through an optically isolated relay board. Latching solenoids are used in preference to continuously energised types because they consume power only during state transitions, a critical property for a solar-powered installation. Software interlocks prevent simultaneous opening of more banks than the supply pipe can support at rated pressure, protecting against pump cavitation.

\subsection{Energy Subsystem}

The energy budget was sized for the worst-case daily consumption profile: 12~hours of active GPU inference at 8.6~W, plus ancillary loads totalling approximately 3.0~W, yielding a total average system draw of 11.6~W and a daily consumption of $\approx$139~Wh. The 100~W monocrystalline PV panel was selected to provide a typical daily yield of $\approx$387~Wh at the study-site latitude (33°N), giving a worst-clear-day surplus of 248~Wh. The 12~V / 50~Ah LiFePO4 battery (usable capacity $\approx$480~Wh) provides approximately 3.5 days of autonomy in the absence of solar input. LiFePO4 was preferred over lead-acid for its deeper permissible discharge, longer cycle life ($>$2000 cycles at 80\% DoD), and superior performance at elevated temperatures above 35\,°C.

\section{Software Architecture}

The software stack is built on the NVIDIA JetPack~4.6 SDK, providing Ubuntu 18.04 (L4T), CUDA~10.2, cuDNN~8.2, and TensorRT~8.2. The application is structured into five loosely coupled services, each managed by \texttt{systemd} to ensure automatic restart on failure or power cycle:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Acquisition Service:} wraps the IMX219 camera via GStreamer/V4L2 and the environmental sensors via I$^2$C, UART, and 1-Wire; publishes frames and telemetry to shared memory queues.
    \item \textbf{Inference Service:} wraps the TensorRT FP16 engine using the TensorRT Python API and PyCUDA; receives frames, pre-processes, runs inference, and publishes detection results.
    \item \textbf{Decision Service:} implements zone aggregation, sensor fusion, Penman-Monteith ET$_0$ estimation, and the fuzzy rule-based prescription logic; publishes per-zone duty cycles.
    \item \textbf{Actuation Service:} receives duty cycles and drives the relay board through RPi.GPIO-compatible GPIO pins; enforces all hardware safety interlocks.
    \item \textbf{Telemetry Service:} serialises zone state, detection summaries, sensor readings, energy status, and system health to a local store-and-forward queue; publishes to the cloud MQTT broker when connectivity is available.
\end{enumerate}

The complete deployment image is encapsulated in a Docker container set, so that a replacement Jetson Nano can be brought online by writing a single SD card image, ensuring operational continuity with minimal downtime.

\section{Vision Model Architecture}

\subsection{YOLOv8n: Architecture and Selection Rationale}

YOLOv8~\cite{Jocher2023} is the eighth major iteration of the YOLO family, released by Ultralytics in January 2023. It unifies object detection, instance segmentation, pose estimation, and image classification in a single framework and is offered in five capacity variants: nano (n), small (s), medium (m), large (l), and extra-large (x). The nano variant, YOLOv8n, is adopted as the primary model in this work for its favourable accuracy-to-compute ratio under the memory and throughput constraints of the Jetson Nano Maxwell GPU.

Architecturally, YOLOv8n comprises three functional blocks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Backbone:} A CSPDarknet-style network organised around the \emph{C2f} (Cross-Stage Partial with 2-layer bottleneck) module, which improves gradient flow through additional shortcut paths with minimal parameter overhead.
    \item \textbf{Neck:} A PANet (Path Aggregation Network) feature-pyramid module that aggregates multi-scale feature maps from the backbone, ensuring the detection head receives a balanced representation of fine and coarse spatial features.
    \item \textbf{Head:} An anchor-free, decoupled detection head with separate classification and regression branches, which improves both localisation precision and training convergence.
\end{itemize}

With approximately 3.2~million parameters and 8.7~GFLOPs at 640$\times$640 input resolution, YOLOv8n achieves a COCO val mAP of $\approx$37.3 at IoU 0.50:0.95---well above the threshold required for reliable detection of the four target classes defined in this work.

\subsection{YOLOv11n: Architectural Advances and Comparative Role}

YOLOv11~\cite{Ultralytics2024}, released in September 2024, introduces two principal architectural changes relative to YOLOv8: (i)~replacement of the C2f backbone module with the more parameter-efficient \emph{C3k2} (Cross-Stage Partial with 3-layer bottleneck, 2 kernels) module; and (ii)~addition of the \emph{C2PSA} (Cross-Stage Partial with Positional Self-Attention) module in the neck, which provides an explicit spatial attention mechanism that improves localisation of small and elongated objects---a relevant benefit for detecting isolated weed plants in dense crop canopies.

\section{Irrigation Decision Logic}

The decision logic translates the structured visual and sensor data into hydraulic commands. It operates as a three-stage pipeline: zone aggregation, state estimation, and prescription generation.

\textbf{Zone Aggregation.}~The field beneath the pivot circle is partitioned into a regular angular-radial grid. As the lateral rotates, the GNSS module reports the pivot angle at 1~Hz. Each YOLOv8 detection bounding box is projected onto the ground plane using the known camera geometry and mounting height to estimate its radial position, then tagged with the concurrent GNSS angle to assign it to a grid zone. Over a configurable temporal window (default: one full rotation), per-zone detection counts, canopy cover fractions, and weed density estimates are accumulated.

\textbf{State Estimation.}~For each zone, the visual summary is combined with: (i)~the most recent soil-moisture reading from the nearest reference probe; (ii)~the reference evapotranspiration $ET_0$ calculated from the BME280 data using the FAO-56 Penman-Monteith equation simplified for hourly data; and (iii)~the accumulated depletion since the last irrigation event. The resulting multi-dimensional state vector is mapped to a discrete water-stress level (0 = well-watered; 3 = severe stress) by a fuzzy rule base.

\textbf{Prescription Generation.}~The water-stress level of each zone is translated into a target irrigation depth, and this depth is encoded as a duty cycle for the corresponding sprinkler bank. A zone at stress level~0 receives no water on the current pass; a zone at level~3 receives the maximum deliverable depth. Intermediate levels are interpolated. The prescription is subjected to hydraulic safety constraints (maximum aggregate flow, maximum daily allocation) and remains overridable by the operator through the web UI.

\section{Cloud Monitoring Dashboard}

The cloud monitoring layer is intentionally decoupled from real-time control. Its architecture rests on three open-source components: (i)~an Eclipse Mosquitto MQTT broker with TLS~1.2 and X.509 per-device certificates; (ii)~InfluxDB 2.x as the time-series data store, exploiting its native high-frequency write path and Flux query language; and (iii)~Grafana as the visualisation and alerting front-end, providing configurable dashboards accessible from a web browser or mobile device.

Dashboard panels include: a live field-overview panel (pivot angle, zone states, soil-moisture, cumulative water delivered); a historical trend panel (daily/weekly water consumption, weed detections, stress events); an alert panel (threshold-crossing notifications for flow anomalies, battery voltage, or sensor faults); and a system-health panel (deployed model version, on-device benchmark metrics, storage utilisation). The dashboard transmits no control commands to the pivot; configuration changes are proposed through the REST~API and applied only after the local safety checks on the Jetson Nano are satisfied.

% ─── 4.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Experimental Setup and Field Deployment}

The prototype was deployed on a four-span centre-pivot with a lateral length of 120~m, operated on an agricultural holding in north-eastern Algeria representative of the semi-arid conditions of the region (mean annual precipitation $<$350~mm; summer temperatures regularly exceeding 40\,°C). The pivot completes one full revolution in approximately 24~hours under full-flow conditions.

The Jetson Nano was housed in an IP65-rated polycarbonate enclosure fitted with a 5~V axial fan for active thermal management. The enclosure was mounted on the first lateral span at a height of 2.5~m above the crop canopy. The Sony IMX219 camera was secured to a custom aluminium bracket at a downward tilt of 30° from horizontal, producing a ground footprint of approximately 1.5--2.0~m width aligned with the direction of lateral travel. The camera was connected to the Jetson Nano via the MIPI CSI-2 interface using a 15-pin FFC.

Environmental sensors (BME280 in radiation-shielded housing; DS18B20 at 10~cm and 30~cm depth) were installed in a weatherproof junction box adjacent to the main enclosure. Two METER TEROS~10 capacitive soil-moisture probes were co-located with the DS18B20 probes in reference zones defined by the pre-deployment soil survey. The U-blox NEO-M8N GNSS module was mounted on the outer face of the enclosure with an unobstructed sky view. The YF-S201 flow meter was inserted into the main irrigation supply pipe upstream of the first bank. All external wiring was routed through IP68-rated cable glands.

The 100~W PV panel was installed at the base of the central tower on a fixed-tilt frame oriented south at the latitude-optimal angle (33°~tilt at 33°N). The MPPT controller, battery, and buck converter were housed in a separate steel enclosure at the tower base, connected to the pivot by a 12~V supply cable run along the lateral structure.

% ─── 4.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Dataset Construction}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Image Acquisition Protocol}

The training dataset was constructed from two sources. The \emph{primary source} consisted of images collected directly from the pivot-mounted camera over multiple revolutions spanning four growth stages of the study crop: (i)~early emergence ($\le$BBCH~19), (ii)~mid-vegetative (BBCH~21--39), (iii)~early reproductive (BBCH~51--69), and (iv)~late maturity (BBCH~81--89). Images were extracted from video at an angular sampling interval corresponding to a ground-level step of approximately 0.5~m. Each frame was timestamped and tagged with the concurrent GNSS angle to enable post-hoc geo-referencing.

To account for illumination variability, acquisition sessions were scheduled across three solar positions: early morning ($<$2~hours after sunrise), midday ($\pm$1~hour of solar noon), and late afternoon ($>$2~hours before sunset), as well as under overcast conditions. Sessions during low-wind, high-dust periods were also included to sample sensor degradation conditions.

The \emph{secondary source} consisted of publicly available agricultural computer-vision datasets---specifically, subsets of the DeepWeeds~\cite{Olsen2019} and Plant Village datasets---selected for visual similarity to the study-site weed and crop species. These images were included to mitigate domain-specific over-fitting and to improve generalisation to weed species under-represented in the primary collection.

\subsection{Annotation Workflow}

All images were annotated using LabelImg with YOLO-format bounding-box output. Disagreements were resolved by a third reviewer. Quality control consisted of a secondary pass to remove mislabelled, blurred, or motion-affected instances.

\subsection{Pre-processing Pipeline}

Images were pre-processed through the following standardised pipeline prior to training:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \emph{Quality filtering:} manual removal of frames with motion blur (Laplacian variance $<$100), severe overexposure ($>$95\% saturated pixels), or underexposure ($<$5\% non-black pixels).
    \item \emph{Histogram equalisation:} applied to images with a mean luminance $<$60 or $>$200 to reduce illumination-induced domain shift.
    \item \emph{Letterbox resize:} all images padded and resized to 640$\times$640 using bilinear interpolation while preserving aspect ratio.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The following metrics were computed on the held-out test set:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{mAP@0.5:} mean Average Precision at IoU threshold 0.50; primary metric for agricultural detection.
    \item \textbf{mAP@0.5:0.95:} mean Average Precision averaged over IoU thresholds from 0.50 to 0.95 in steps of 0.05; standard COCO metric.
    \item \textbf{Per-class AP@0.5:} class-level precision-recall curves and area under curve.
    \item \textbf{Precision and Recall:} at the default confidence threshold of 0.25.
    \item \textbf{F1-score:} harmonic mean of precision and recall; reported per class.
    \item \textbf{Inference latency:} mean and 99th-percentile latency in milliseconds.
    \item \textbf{Throughput:} frames per second.
\end{itemize}

% ─── 4.4 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Edge Deployment to Jetson Nano}

The trained YOLOv8n checkpoint was exported to ONNX (opset~17) using the Ultralytics \texttt{model.export()} API with \texttt{dynamic=True} to permit variable batch sizes. The ONNX graph was inspected with Netron to verify operator compatibility with TensorRT~8.2. TensorRT FP16 and INT8 engines were compiled directly on the target Jetson Nano using the \texttt{trtexec} command-line utility to ensure that the GPU-specific kernel selection and memory layout optimisations are performed for the exact hardware in deployment. The INT8 calibration dataset comprised 200 images representative of deployment conditions, processed through the \texttt{IInt8Calibrator} interface.

Inference is served by a Python process using the TensorRT~8 Python API and PyCUDA for explicit GPU memory management. The inference pipeline performs: (i)~BGR-to-RGB conversion and letterbox padding; (ii)~normalisation to [0,1] as FP16; (iii)~host-to-device copy to CUDA pinned memory; (iv)~engine execution; (v)~device-to-host copy; and (vi)~batched non-maximum suppression with confidence threshold 0.25 and NMS IoU threshold 0.45.

% ─── 4.5 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Android Companion Application}

A secondary Android application was developed to provide farm operators with a handheld diagnostic tool. The application was implemented in Kotlin using the CameraX API for camera access and integrates a TFLite INT8 model exported via the Ultralytics TFLite export pipeline. Two principal views are provided: (i)~a live-detection view overlaying bounding boxes on the camera feed in real time; and (ii)~a telemetry view displaying zone states and sensor readings received from the Jetson Nano via the local MQTT broker over farm Wi-Fi.

% ─── 4.6 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Baseline Systems and Controlled Comparisons}

Three baseline configurations were established for comparison:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Baseline B1 --- Conventional Fixed-Rate Pivot:} uniform irrigation depth across the entire field at every pass, replicating the standard practice on the study farm prior to the project.
    \item \textbf{Baseline B2 --- Sensor-Only Smart Scheduling:} irrigation prescription derived exclusively from the two soil-moisture probes, without any visual input. This isolates the incremental value of the computer-vision modality.
    \item \textbf{Baseline B3 --- Simulated Cloud-Based Vision:} camera frames transmitted over a 4G link to a remote server running YOLOv8n in FP32 mode; detections returned to the pivot controller. This baseline quantifies the latency, bandwidth, and robustness trade-offs of edge versus cloud deployment.
\end{enumerate}

% ─── 4.7 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Evaluation Criteria}

The prototype is evaluated against the following criteria, each directly addressing one of the five research questions posed in Chapter~I:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Detection accuracy} (RQ1, RQ2): mAP@0.5 and mAP@0.5:0.95 on the test set under FP32, FP16, and INT8 deployment for both YOLOv8n and YOLOv11n.
    \item \textbf{Inference latency and throughput} (RQ1, RQ3): mean and P99 latency; fps; end-to-end perception-to-actuation latency.
    \item \textbf{Energy consumption} (RQ4): average system power draw in W; daily energy consumption in Wh; minimum battery state of charge during the evaluation period.
    \item \textbf{Water savings} (RQ4): percentage reduction in delivered water volume relative to each baseline.
    \item \textbf{Communication bandwidth} (RQ4): average MQTT uplink data rate to the cloud.
    \item \textbf{Operational robustness} (RQ4, RQ5): percentage of scheduled revolutions completed without inference interruption; number and nature of manual interventions.
\end{enumerate}

These results support the recommendation to adopt YOLOv11n as the primary architecture in the next iteration of the system.

\textbf{Per-class performance.} The Crop and Bare Soil classes consistently achieve mAP@0.5 above 90\% across all deployment configurations, reflecting their large spatial extent and high inter-class contrast. The Weed class achieves moderate performance ($\approx$82--85\% mAP@0.5) owing to the diversity of weed species and the frequent partial occlusion of individual plants beneath the crop canopy at reproductive growth stages. The Stressed Plant class achieves the lowest performance ($\approx$75--78\% mAP@0.5), limited by the visual subtlety of early-stage stress symptoms and the relatively small number of labelled stress instances in the dataset.

\section{Inference Latency and Real-Time Performance}

The end-to-end perception-to-actuation latency---from camera frame capture to relay-board command---was measured at 38~ms for the FP16 YOLOv8n production configuration, of which 32~ms is attributed to the TensorRT inference pipeline and the remaining 6~ms to zone aggregation and decision logic execution. This corresponds to an angular displacement of the pivot lateral of approximately $3.2 \times 10^{-4}$~m at the lateral tip---negligible relative to the $\approx$15~m spatial footprint of each sprinkler bank.

Camera frame delivery at 30~fps was sustained throughout the evaluation season under nominal thermal conditions ($<$40\,°C enclosure temperature). Thermal throttling of the Jetson Nano GPU clock was observed on 14 days when ambient air temperature exceeded 42\,°C, resulting in intermittent frame rate reductions to $\approx$22~fps for periods of 30--90~minutes during the hottest portion of the afternoon. The addition of the active cooling fan (a design revision implemented after an initial fan-free field trial) reduced the frequency of throttling events from daily to occasional, confirming the necessity of forced-air cooling in the Algerian summer.

Baseline B3 (simulated cloud inference) incurred a round-trip latency of 380--520~ms over the 4G link, and was unavailable for 17\% of the evaluation period owing to cellular network outages. The edge-AI approach thus achieves a latency advantage of approximately 12$\times$ and, critically, 100\% availability during network outages.

\section{Comparison with Prior Work}

The water savings achieved (18--23\% relative to a fixed-rate baseline) are within the range of 10--25\% reported across the VRI literature for comparable arid and semi-arid environments~\cite{Evans2012}, and at the upper bound of the subset of studies combining computer vision with pivot-mounted sensing. The inference latency of 32~ms for FP16 YOLOv8n is lower than latencies reported in the majority of comparable edge-AI agricultural deployments~\cite{Torres2022}, confirming the effectiveness of the TensorRT optimisation pipeline. The system power draw of 11.6~W is consistent with published Jetson Nano deployments running inference workloads of comparable complexity (10--14~W range). The ability to sustain this power draw from a 100~W PV installation with full energy autonomy has not, to the authors' knowledge, been reported for a pivot-mounted agricultural edge-AI system.

% ─── 5.6 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Qualitative Field Observations}

Three qualitative observations from the deployment are noteworthy. First, the farm operator's acceptance of the system and their reported confidence in the prescriptions, attributed to the Grafana dashboard's transparency, suggests that \emph{explainability} of the decision logic is as important as technical performance for technology adoption in agricultural settings. Second, the maintenance burden of the deployed system was light: the principal recurring tasks were weekly camera-lens cleaning during dusty conditions and monthly PV panel surface inspection. No hardware failures were recorded over the evaluation period.

Third, the visual pipeline demonstrated operational value beyond water saving in one notable instance: the system detected a localised zone of stress symptoms approximately 48~hours before the symptoms became visible to a human observer walking the field. Investigation traced the stress to a partially blocked sprinkler nozzle in the affected lateral section, which was cleared before the stress progressed to a yield-affecting level. This incident illustrates the potential of continuous automated monitoring as a fault-detection tool, a use case not originally primary in the system design.

% ─── 5.7 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Research Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The following limitations must be explicitly acknowledged, as they constrain the generalisability of the conclusions:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{L\arabic*.}]
    \item \textbf{Single-season, single-site evaluation.} The evaluation was conducted over one growing season at one field, limiting the statistical robustness of the water-saving estimates and the generalisability of detection results to other crop species, weed communities, or agroclimatic zones.

    \item \textbf{Dataset size and diversity.} The annotated dataset covers a limited number of weed species and growth-stage combinations. The stressed-plant class in particular is susceptible to over-fitting owing to its lower instance count relative to crop and bare-soil instances.

    \item \textbf{Illumination and environmental constraints.} Detection performance degrades under direct overhead sunlight (contrast saturation), heavy dust (optical scattering), and nocturnal conditions (sensor noise limit). These constitute operational constraints rather than system failures but should be quantified in future work.

    \item \textbf{Energy validation restricted to summer months.} The energy budget analysis predicts autonomous winter operation, but this was not empirically validated during the evaluation period.

    \item \textbf{Counterfactual water-saving estimation.} The 18--23\% water-saving figure compares the Smart Pivot's actual delivery with a counterfactual fixed-rate delivery computed from the farm's historical schedule, rather than a concurrent controlled experiment with a parallel fixed-rate pivot. This introduces uncertainty that can be reduced by a split-field experiment in future work.

    \item \textbf{Thermal throttling under extreme heat.} GPU clock throttling occurred during peak afternoon temperatures above 42\,°C, reducing throughput to $\approx$22~fps. While actuation correctness was not compromised, this represents a performance boundary under extreme Saharan summer conditions.

    \item \textbf{Cybersecurity.} The current security posture (TLS transport encryption and X.509 device certificates) constitutes a minimum baseline. Vulnerabilities to replay attacks, firmware injection, and cloud-broker compromise have not been formally assessed and represent an open risk for operational deployment.

    \item \textbf{False detections in edge cases.} Qualitative review of inference outputs identified occasional false-positive weed detections in image regions containing irrigation water droplets forming bead patterns on soil, and occasional false-positive stress detections associated with deep shadows rather than plant physiological symptoms. These edge cases were infrequent but should be addressed through targeted augmentation in future training iterations.
\end{enumerate}

% ─── 5.8 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Integrated Discussion}

Taken together, the results provide positive answers to research questions RQ1--RQ4. The YOLOv8n FP16 TensorRT engine achieves real-time performance at agronomically acceptable accuracy on the Jetson Nano (RQ1). The four-class detection framework reliably captures the agronomically most important field-state indicators under the majority of operating conditions encountered (RQ2). The 38~ms end-to-end latency is orders of magnitude smaller than the hydraulic response time of the irrigation system and is therefore fully compatible with closed-loop control (RQ3). The edge-AI architecture demonstrates clear quantitative advantages over cloud-based alternatives in latency, bandwidth, and operational availability (RQ4).

Regarding RQ5 (affordability), the prototype bill-of-materials cost is estimated at under USD~400 in 2025 prices for the electronic and photovoltaic components, excluding civil works and pivot integration. While non-trivial for an individual smallholder, this cost is consistent with a cooperative or extension-service deployment model and is expected to decrease with advancing commoditisation of edge-AI hardware.

\section{Summary of Contributions}

This dissertation has designed, implemented, and evaluated the Smart Pivot: a centre-pivot irrigation system that mounts an NVIDIA Jetson Nano edge-AI module on the rotating lateral to perform real-time YOLOv8-based visual inference and derive spatially resolved, connectivity-independent variable-rate irrigation prescriptions. The following concrete achievements were demonstrated:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{C\arabic*.}]
    \item \textbf{End-to-end edge-AI irrigation prototype.} A complete, field-deployable hardware-software system integrating camera-based visual perception, multi-sensor fusion, fuzzy decision logic, latching-solenoid actuation, solar power autonomy, and cloud telemetry---all on a single Jetson Nano at the pivot lateral.

    \item \textbf{TensorRT quantisation pipeline.} A fully documented five-stage pipeline (PyTorch FP32 $\to$ ONNX $\to$ TensorRT FP16 $\to$ TensorRT INT8 $\to$ on-device validation) converting a trained YOLOv8n checkpoint to a real-time engine at 31~fps on the Maxwell GPU with $<$0.5 mAP@0.5 degradation.

    \item \textbf{Closed-loop VRI with 38~ms end-to-end latency.} A perception-decision-actuation cycle meeting the latency budget of centre-pivot irrigation control without cloud connectivity.

    \item \textbf{18--23\% water saving in the field.} Measured reduction in delivered water volume relative to a conventional fixed-rate baseline over a complete growing season.

    \item \textbf{Full energy autonomy at 11.6~W.} Demonstrated continuous operation sustained by a 100~W PV installation with a minimum battery SoC of 68\% during a 3-day overcast period.

    \item \textbf{Architectural comparison of YOLOv8n and YOLOv11n on embedded hardware.} A rigorous FP32/FP16/INT8 benchmark providing the community with reference latency, accuracy, and energy figures for these architectures on the Jetson Nano.

    \item \textbf{Android companion application.} A TFLite INT8 inference application providing portable field diagnostics via a commercial smartphone.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scientific and Engineering Contributions to Knowledge}

The contributions of this work to the scientific literature are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{First documented end-to-end field evaluation} of a YOLOv8-based edge-AI system for closed-loop centre-pivot VRI, bridging the gap between isolated agricultural computer-vision demonstrations and operationally deployed precision irrigation systems.
    \item \textbf{Rigorous quantisation study} of FP16 and INT8 TensorRT deployment of YOLOv8n and YOLOv11n on the Jetson Nano, with detailed per-class accuracy, latency, and energy data that can serve as a reproducible reference for future edge-AI agricultural deployments.
    \item \textbf{Energy-autonomy validation} of a solar-powered, IP65-protected pivot-mounted edge-AI system through a full summer growing season in a semi-arid Algerian climate.
    \item \textbf{Quantified comparison} of edge-AI and cloud-AI architectures in the specific operational context of pivot-mounted computer vision, providing a field-measured dataset of latency, bandwidth, and availability trade-offs.
    \item \textbf{First demonstration} of an integrated smart-pivot system validated in the agroclimatic context of semi-arid Algeria, addressing a geographically specific gap documented in the review literature.
\end{itemize}

\section{Practical Applications}

The Smart Pivot system is designed for practical deployment and targets the following user communities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Medium- to large-scale farmers and agricultural cooperatives operating centre-pivot systems in arid and semi-arid regions of Algeria and the broader southern Mediterranean basin.
    \item Agricultural research stations and university experimental farms requiring a reproducible, open-source platform for agronomic experimentation under real-world precision irrigation conditions.
    \item Extension services and government agricultural bodies seeking to demonstrate and scale affordable, connectivity-independent precision irrigation technology in water-constrained regions.
\end{itemize}

\section{Directions for Future Research}
\label{sec:future}

The results and limitations identified in Section~\ref{sec:limitations} point to six priority directions for future work:

\subsection{Multispectral and Thermal Imaging Fusion}
The RGB camera provides an effective low-cost perception modality but is unable to detect pre-symptomatic water stress, which manifests primarily in the thermal infrared channel as stomatal closure-induced canopy temperature elevation before visible wilting occurs. Integration of a low-cost thermal module (e.g., FLIR Lepton 3.5) with the existing RGB camera, and training a multimodal fusion model jointly on both channels, is expected to substantially improve the stressed-plant detector's sensitivity, particularly during the latent stress phase of practical agronomic importance.

\subsection{Expanded and Diversified Dataset}
The current dataset's limited species coverage and single growing season constrain model generalisability. A systematic multi-year, multi-field, multi-crop data collection programme---potentially involving collaboration with Algerian agricultural research institutes and extension services---would support training of a robust generalised model reducing the need for per-deployment fine-tuning.

\subsection{Federated Learning for Cross-Farm Model Improvement}
Once a fleet of Smart Pivot units is operational across multiple farms, the federated learning framework of McMahan et al.~\cite{McMahan2017} provides a principled mechanism for collaborative model improvement without sharing raw imagery. Each device contributes gradient updates computed on its local data; the server aggregates these to improve the global model, which is then redistributed via OTA update. This preserves data sovereignty while enabling the model to learn from a corpus of field experience far larger than any individual farm can provide.

\subsection{Migration to Next-Generation Edge Hardware}
The Jetson Orin Nano (40~TOPS, JetPack~6) and the Hailo-8 accelerator provide $5\times$ to $10\times$ greater AI throughput within similar power envelopes. Migration to these platforms would unlock the use of larger, more accurate models (e.g., YOLOv8s or multispectral architectures) at real-time frame rates, enabling richer decision logic and support for additional agronomic tasks such as canopy-cover estimation and disease detection.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Prescription Optimisation}
The current rule-based decision logic is static and depends on agronomically specified thresholds. Replacing it with a reinforcement learning agent---trained in a high-fidelity crop-growth and soil-water simulation environment and then fine-tuned on-device through interaction with the real field---would allow the system to discover non-obvious irrigation strategies that maximise season-level yield rather than minimising per-pass water consumption, potentially uncovering agronomy-level trade-offs not captured by expert rules.

\subsection{Longitudinal Agronomic and Economic Evaluation}
A multi-year, multi-field evaluation tracking yield, water-use efficiency ($\text{WUE} = \text{yield} / \text{applied water}$), soil salinity dynamics, and net economic return is required to produce the evidence base needed to justify widespread adoption and to inform policy support for edge-AI precision irrigation in Algeria. The experimental protocols established in this dissertation provide the methodological foundation for such a study.
